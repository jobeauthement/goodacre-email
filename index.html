Farm Share Week 10!
Hello, Farm Share members!
We are entering the height of summer now - corn, melons, peppers, and tomatoes abound or are getting ready to explode in volume! It's a wondrous, though very busy time in our warehouse. Here at TGA, the majority of this abundance comes from BIPOC farmers who rent their land. While some farmers are content with where they are at in their business, and don't necessarily want to expand a lot or own their own land, many do. The process of finding land is long and difficult, and of course, the expense of buying it is often inhibitive. According to the USDA, access to land, and capital, is largest hurdle for emerging farmers. A few of the farmers we work with have recently finalized purchases on their farms, and the process has taken years for several of them.
Historically, it has been harder, or nearly impossible, for BIPOC (and specifically Black farmers in the US) to secure loans from the USDA, and the majority immigrant farmers we work with today have added barriers of language, different cultural norms around business, and having to start anew to the already difficult job of farming. Owning your own land allows for building generational wealth, and currently mostly white farmers own their own farmland. (Add to this the fact that extremely rich people like Bill Gates - who last year became the largest private landowner in the US - are buying up farmland, which brings up a whole other host of issues like consolidation of land and wealth.) It also means that farmers have more control over how they farm. Many farmers we work with would like to do more building up of their soil, or growing perennial crops, but with the uncertainty of renting and not knowing when you might have to leave, these more expensive, longer-term investments don't make a lot of sense.
Your support of TGA already means you are helping small farmers grow their business and further their farm goals, but there are lots of great organizations here in MN helping farmers find land, navigate the banking and business aspects of farming, and finance purchases, like the Land Stewardship Project, Renewing the Countryside, the Hmong American Partnership, and the Mill City Farmers Market Charitable Fund. Check out their work, and if land access is an interesting topic to you, there are tons of organizations around the US that are doing similar work (look up some land back orgs as an adjacent topic!).

In the Box This Week
1. Cabbage from The Early Birds and Featherstone Farm
The first cabbage of the season is here! Try this tasty creamy coleslaw recipe this week or this one which features cucumbers and jalapeño. If you're looking to fire up the grill, try this recipe for grilled cabbage steaks. Another winner is this caramelized cabbage and leek pasta, pair with a nice glass of Sangiovese.
2. Cucumbers from Agua Gorda Farm, Choua Yang, Pheng Yang, and The Early Birds
If you are looking for new cuke recipes, try my fave: cucumber syrup! Just combine sliced cucumbers and sugar overnight in the fridge and strain in the morning! Makes a nice refreshing drink mixed with fizzy water and mint or basil. If you're over cucumber salad, try making these cucumber cups, they are the perfect little edible bowl for tuna salad, chicken salad or a creamy cheesy filling.
3. Jalapeños from The Carrillo Brothers and Santa Rosa Farm
A classic way to go is jalapeño poppers, but you can tone them down a bit with jalapeño popper mac n' cheese. Give your corn fritters a kick, or add a lil something to rice bowls with jalapeño guac. If you've got extra watermelon laying around, mix them into a cucumber jalapeño salad.
4. Leeks from Hope Organics and Pheng Yang
Leeks originated in Asia, were common on the menu in ancient Egypt and Rome, and are the national symbol of the Welsh! Read more about the history of leeks here. We're big fans of potato and leek soup but you can still enjoy this dynamic duo in a celery gratin to stir it up. You can also sauté leeks with cabbage for a side dish with meats.
5. Sweet Corn from Healthy Greens Farm, Santa Rosa Farm, and The Early Bird
There are so many ways to enjoy corn. If you don't have a grill, you can do baked on the cob! Or cool down with sweet corn gazpacho and a side of creamy corn salad. Corn will also pair perfectly with leeks and chard so try this recipe for a Leek, Chard, and Corn Flatbreadwhich. Fun Fact: this week's sweet corn is one of six major varieties of corn (sweet corn contains low starch content) plus corn can be grown on every continent except Antarctica!
6. Swiss Chard from Dawn 2 Dusk Farm and The Early Birds
Chard is a slightly bitter, luscious green with large leaves and beautifully colorful stems. Did you know chard is in the same veggie family as beets and amaranth? Have a delicious savory breakfast of Lentils with Cucumbers, Chard and Poached Egg, or try your hand at homemade Clam, Chard, and Bacon Pizza. If you're up at the lake this week, how about an Almond-Crusted Trout with White Grits and Swiss Chard?

Featured Recipe: Leek, Chard and Corn Flatbread
These three ingredients cooked together play off each other fantastically; the leeks get deep and sweet; the chard wilts but retains its bulk and the corn kernels crunch across the top. Make tons of this flatbread before summer corn becomes a distant memory. Recipe provided by Smitten Kitchen - yields 2 large (9x13ish inches) flatbreads.
Ingredients
  • About 1 1/2 pounds pizza dough (from two of these, or store-bought)
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable or olive oil
  • 1 tablespoon unsalted butter
  • 3 large leeks or a combination of leeks and onions (about 1 1/2 pounds)
  • 1 bunch of chard or about 6 cups (6 ounces) of leaves, cut into 1/2-inch ribbons
  • 1/2 teaspoon table salt
  • 1 cup fresh corn kernels (cut from 1 to 2 medium ears corn)
  • Freshly ground black pepper
  • 1 tablespoon unsalted butter
  • Cornmeal, for sprinkling baking surface
  • 4-ounce goat cheese (or any cheese), cold
Directions
  1. Trim the ends off your leeks and halve them lengthwise. Cut them crosswise into 1/2-inch half-rings. Fill a medium bowl with very cold water and drop in sliced leeks. Swish them around with your fingers, letting any sandy dirt fall to the bottom. Scoop out the leeks and drain them briefly on a towel, but no need to get them fully dry. Do the same with your chard ribbons, but you can leave the leaves on towels until they’re nearly fully dry, while you cook the leeks (or leeks and onions).
  2. Heat your oven to 450 degrees.
  3. Heat a large, heavy skillet over medium heat. Once hot, add butter and oil and once they’re fully melted and a bit sizzly, add the leek slices. Reduce heat to low, cover with a lid and cook leek for 10 to 13 minutes, stirring occasionally.
  4. Raise heat back to medium, add the chard ribbons and cook until they wilt, about 4 minutes. Season mixture with salt and freshly ground black pepper, adding more if needed.
  5. Finally, add your corn kernels, cooking them with the leeks and chard for just another minute.
  6. Sprinkle two baking sheets with cornmeal. If you have pesky old baking trays like I do, and your breads really like to stick to them, I find things will release more reliably if you first lightly spray them with an oil before sprinkling on the cornmeal
  7. Roll or stretch half your dough into a rectangular-ish shape (flatbreads are prettiest when they’re irregularly shaped, in my humble opinion) and arrange it on the prepared sheet. Spread half the leek-corn-chard mixture on it. Sprinkle it with half the goat cheese, crumbled into small bits.
  8. Bake flatbread in oven for 10 to 15 minutes, until the edges of the bread begin to brown slightly (they might brown more deeply in a better oven than they did in my lousy one). Repeat with remaining dough, filling and goat cheese.
  9. To serve, slide each flatbread onto a cutting board and cut into 8 rectangles. Serve immediately.

Farmer Spotlight: Javier Garcia
Meet Javier Garcia, a provider of your cucumbers this week! Javier is one of a small but growing number of Latino farmers in Minnesota. According to the agricultural census, Minnesota is home to about 112,000 farmers. Of those, only 650 or 0.006% identify as Hispanic or Latino.
Javier arrived to the U.S. in 1993 from his home in the Mexican state of Michoacán. “I never imagined I would own land here. But it’s given me the motivation (to keep going) because in Mexico we didn’t have land where we could grow crops,” Javier says. After working several years harvesting crops in California’s Central Valley followed by landscaping work in Los Angeles, Garcia moved to Minnesota. The idea for Agua Gorda, named after his hometown in Mexico, was planted in 2011. He now owns 54 acres in Long Prairie, Minnesota - equivalent to roughly 54 football fields.
This year’s crop includes rows of onion, Roma tomatoes and zucchini. The largest section is dedicated to honeydew melons which Javier is contracted to sell to schools in Minneapolis and St. Paul. Although he owns and farms his land, Javier still works at a dairy farm — full-time in the winter and part-time during the farming season — in order to make ends meet. When it’s time to harvest, his adult children and grandchildren all pitch in. But he admitted, he also gets some unwanted help. “The deer also help us harvest."
